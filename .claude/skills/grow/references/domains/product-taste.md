# Product Taste

Building the right thing. When anyone can build your product in a weekend, design judgment and customer empathy are the moat. Taste is pattern recognition developed through experience, not unteachable genius.

## User Empathy

Understanding who you're building for through conversation, not assumption. The discipline of talking to users before, during, and after building.

- **Level 0:** Builds for themselves or an imagined user. No user conversations. Assumes their own experience is representative.
- **Level 1:** Knows user research matters but does it sporadically or not at all. Relies on assumptions, indirect signals, or what AI suggests users might want. Asks leading questions that confirm existing beliefs.
- **Level 2:** Talks to real users regularly. Distinguishes stated wants from actual needs — observes what people do, not just what they say. Conversation patterns drive product decisions.
- **Level 3:** Systematic discovery practice: structured interviews using validated frameworks (Mom Test, JTBD), triangulated across multiple signal sources (support tickets, usage data, competitor reviews, sales objections). Can identify latent needs users can't articulate. Uses AI to analyze and synthesize research but not to replace direct conversation.
- **Level 4:** User research practices others adopt. Teaches founders how to listen. Designs research processes that scale with the product.
- **Level 5:** Advances the field's understanding of user empathy in the AI era.

## Value Clarity

Articulating what you do and why it matters in one sentence that makes strangers care. When competitors can clone features in a weekend, clear positioning becomes existential.

- **Level 0:** Can't explain what the product does without jargon or a demo. No clear audience.
- **Level 1:** Has a description but it's vague, technical, or tries to appeal to everyone. Hasn't tested it with real strangers. Confuses features with value.
- **Level 2:** Describes product and audience in one sentence a stranger immediately gets. Tested with real people, not just polished internally. Knows the difference between what the product does and why someone would care.
- **Level 3:** Positioning is deliberate and evidence-based: specific audience, specific problem, specific advantage that survives a "weekend clone" test. Can articulate why users choose this over alternatives — including doing nothing. Value proposition evolves with user feedback, not founder attachment.
- **Level 4:** Positioning frameworks others adopt. Helps founders find clarity in their own products. Track record of positioning that drives acquisition.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the community thinks about value communication and positioning.

## Design Judgment

Visual consistency, information architecture, UX decisions that get past AI's 80% quality default. Intentionality and delight over generic competence. When every AI-built app converges on the same Tailwind/Shadcn aesthetic, deliberate design becomes a trust signal.

- **Level 0:** No visual awareness. Ships whatever AI generates — default fonts, default colors, inconsistent spacing, stock imagery.
- **Level 1:** Notices when something looks "off" but can't diagnose why or fix it systematically. Makes ad-hoc design tweaks without a system. Product looks like every other AI-generated app.
- **Level 2:** Notices when AI output looks generic and fixes it. Maintains consistent visual language — typography, color, spacing — and makes deliberate UX choices past the default. Product has at least one intentional design decision that distinguishes it from the sea of sameness.
- **Level 3:** Has a working design system (even if simple): defined tokens, consistent components, deliberate interaction design. Understands why design decisions work, not just what looks good. Catches AI design drift — generated components deviating from established patterns. Accessibility is considered, not an afterthought.
- **Level 4:** Design quality others notice and emulate. Visual identity that functions as brand. Design judgment extends to product architecture, not just surface aesthetics.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the community thinks about design in AI-built products.

## Feature Discipline

What to build, what to skip, what to kill. When building is cheap and AI can generate features in minutes, the temptation is to build everything. Discipline is knowing that three well-executed features beat ten mediocre ones.

- **Level 0:** Builds whatever comes to mind or whatever AI makes easy. No prioritization. Feature list grows without constraint.
- **Level 1:** Has a rough sense of what matters but can't say no — especially when AI makes adding one more thing nearly free. Ships features without validating demand. Confuses feature count with product quality.
- **Level 2:** Has a system for what to skip or kill. Ships core features well rather than everything AI makes possible. Can articulate why specific features were excluded. Uses the test: if you can't launch without it, it's a must-have — everything else is scope creep.
- **Level 3:** Prioritization is evidence-based: usage data, user feedback, and strategic alignment — not excitement or implementation ease. Actively kills features that aren't earning their complexity. Accounts for maintenance burden: features are cheap to build but expensive to keep.
- **Level 4:** Feature discipline others adopt. Designs prioritization frameworks for teams. Track record of products that do less, better.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the community thinks about product scope and restraint.

## PMF Judgment

Reading the signal on product-market fit. Knowing when you've found it, when you're fooling yourself, and when it's collapsing. PMF is a spectrum, not a checkbox — and in the AI era, it can collapse overnight when a platform absorbs your use case.

- **Level 0:** No concept of product-market fit. Ships and hopes. Interprets any usage as validation.
- **Level 1:** Knows PMF matters but measures it with vanity metrics — signups, page views, social media engagement. Can't distinguish the "AI demo effect" (impressive first use, no return visits) from genuine demand.
- **Level 2:** Tracks specific PMF signals: retention, organic growth, willingness to pay. Distinguishes market pull (users seeking you out, word-of-mouth, unprompted expansion) from self-generated building momentum (shipping features into silence). Watches for PMF collapse risk — platforms or competitors absorbing the use case.
- **Level 3:** PMF measurement is systematic: cohort retention curves, Sean Ellis test (40%+ "very disappointed"), CAC payback period, usage by segment. Knows which use cases drive real retention vs. casual experimentation. Evaluates moats beyond features — distribution, data, workflow lock-in, brand. Distinguishes durable revenue from experimental revenue.
- **Level 4:** PMF judgment others trust. Helps founders read their own signals honestly. Pattern recognition across markets and products.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the field thinks about product-market fit in the AI era.
