# Distribution

Getting people to use what you built. The #1 AI builder failure mode is "shipped but nobody uses it." AI compressed building to days; audience-building still takes months. Distribution is half the product.

## Web Presence & SEO

Existing on the internet and being found. Landing pages, search optimization, analytics, conversion. "Being found" now means being cited by AI engines as much as ranking on Google — AI Overviews appear in a quarter of searches, and most of those clicks never reach your site unless you're the cited source.

- **Level 0:** No web presence beyond maybe a GitHub repo. No landing page, no analytics, not findable by potential users.
- **Level 1:** Has a landing page but it's unclear, doesn't communicate value quickly, or isn't optimized for search. No analytics beyond maybe total visits. Doesn't understand how people find the product.
- **Level 2:** Has a landing page that communicates value clearly, tracks traffic sources, and is findable by someone searching for what the product does. Understands basic SEO — title tags, meta descriptions, content that answers the questions users actually ask.
- **Level 3:** SEO strategy is deliberate: topic clusters for topical authority, content structured for AI citation (direct answers early, fact density, schema markup), landing pages optimized for conversion (single objective, clear reading level). Tracks which sources drive signups, not just traffic. Understands GEO — how AI search engines select sources to cite — and optimizes for it.
- **Level 4:** SEO and web presence practices others adopt. Programmatic SEO at scale. Measurable organic growth as a primary acquisition channel.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the community thinks about discoverability in the AI search era.

## Content Strategy

Building in public, audience development, teaching what you learn. The long game that compounds. Content is distribution — the most durable acquisition channel a solo founder has.

- **Level 0:** No content practice. Product exists but the builder is invisible.
- **Level 1:** Shares sporadically — launch announcements, occasional updates. No consistent cadence. Content is self-promotional rather than valuable to the audience. Doesn't know what resonates or why.
- **Level 2:** Maintains consistent content cadence on at least one platform. Shares real work — building decisions, lessons, mistakes — that visibly contributes to signups or awareness. Understands that people follow builders for perspective, not just information.
- **Level 3:** Content practice is strategic: primary platform chosen for audience fit, supporting channels for repurposing, owned channel (newsletter) for direct relationships independent of algorithms. Practices strategic transparency — shares aggressively for traction early, protects competitive advantages as they emerge. Older content continues driving traffic and signups without maintenance.
- **Level 4:** Content strategy others study and emulate. Creates compounding content loops that drive sustainable acquisition. Audience is a genuine distribution moat.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the community thinks about content as distribution.

## Channel Focus

Going deep on 1-2 channels rather than broadcasting everywhere. Manual-first validation before scaling. The biggest distribution mistake is trying to be everywhere — picking the right channel and committing for 90 days matters more than optimizing across five.

- **Level 0:** No channel strategy. Posts randomly across platforms with no consistency or intent.
- **Level 1:** Present on multiple platforms but spread thin. No clear primary channel. Activity is sporadic and unmeasured. Follows trends rather than going where users already are.
- **Level 2:** Has chosen 1-2 channels based on where users are, is consistently active, and has evidence of which drives results. Maintains one owned channel (email) independent of any platform. Can explain why these channels over alternatives.
- **Level 3:** Channel selection is data-informed: knows CAC and conversion by channel, tests new channels with time-boxed experiments, measures channel-specific metrics beyond vanity numbers. Understands product-channel fit — certain products naturally fit certain channels. Repurposes content across supporting channels without diluting primary effort.
- **Level 4:** Channel strategy others adopt. Identifies emerging channels early. Designs cross-channel systems that compound.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the community thinks about channel selection and focus.

## Community Building

Cultivating relationships that become a moat AI can't replicate. Design partners, user communities, the relational advantage. AI cannot manufacture authentic social proof, real usage, or genuine relationships at scale — which makes community the most defensible distribution channel.

- **Level 0:** No community relationships. Users are anonymous data points. No feedback loop.
- **Level 1:** Has some users but no direct relationships. Participates in communities occasionally but mainly to promote. No design partners or early adopters giving honest feedback.
- **Level 2:** Has direct relationships with early users who give real feedback. Participates meaningfully in at least one relevant community — providing value, not just promoting. Has or is developing design partners who shape product direction.
- **Level 3:** Community is a deliberate growth asset: design partners representing the target audience, active presence where buyers congregate, word-of-mouth as a measurable acquisition channel. Referred users retain better and cost less than paid acquisition. Community feedback directly shapes the roadmap. Users create content, templates, or integrations that distribute the product organically.
- **Level 4:** Community-building practices others adopt. Designs ecosystems (templates, plugins, integrations) that generate organic distribution. Creates community structures that scale.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the community thinks about relationship-driven distribution.

## Growth Systems

Repeatable acquisition, compounding loops, distribution that grows without constant manual effort. Growth loops beat funnels — loops compound while funnels reset every month.

- **Level 0:** No growth system. Relies entirely on launch spikes or manual outreach for every user. Each customer requires the same effort as the first.
- **Level 1:** Has tried various acquisition tactics but nothing repeatable. Growth is a series of one-off experiments rather than a system. Doesn't track funnel metrics or understand unit economics.
- **Level 2:** Has at least one acquisition path that works without constant manual effort. Understands basic funnel metrics — where users come from, where they drop off, what converts. Knows CAC well enough to tell whether growth is sustainable.
- **Level 3:** Growth is systematic: multiple compounding loops (referral, content, integration, community) rather than linear campaigns. Tracks LTV:CAC (targeting 3:1+), cohort retention, and channel-specific economics. Product has built-in distribution mechanics — sharing, referral, or network effects. Experiments are structured with hypotheses and measurable outcomes.
- **Level 4:** Growth systems others adopt. Designs compounding loops that drive sustainable acquisition across products. Track record of repeatable growth engineering.
- **Level 5:** Advances how the field thinks about growth systems for small teams.
